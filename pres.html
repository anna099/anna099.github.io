The homo sacer can be understood as an ‘enemy’ in Schmitt’s sense: an outsider, an absence that is required for the smooth running of the system as a whole.

The concepts of bios and zoe first appeared in Aristotle. They allow us to interpret the picture on the first slide.

Slide 4

Agamben notes that in Hobbes’s De Cive, it is the body’s capacity to be killed that serves as the foudnation of political equality.

But Agamben is not Hobbes: he is not merely interested in a historical situation where ‘every man is Enemy to every man’;

instead, he is interested in the mdoern phenomenon whereby ‘whole victim groups are made available to misuse by being categorised out of the body politic’.

Slide 5

His understanding of totalitarianism makes him stronger than Foucault.

Page 120, Agamben argues that Arendt’s work did not fully realise the implications of biopower.

Foucault’s work, on the other hand, couldn’t comprehend how modern democracy could turn into totalitarianism.

Nazi propaganda: Lebensunwertes Leben (life unworthy of life)

Here, Agamben is comparable to Adorno and Horkheimer, for whom the Holocaust was not an excpetion, but the logical or dialectical outcome of modernity.
